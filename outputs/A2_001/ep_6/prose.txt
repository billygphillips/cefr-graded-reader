Cale Street was quiet. A few cars sat outside the shops. Luca walked fast. The paper in his hand had Ray's address — number fourteen, above the hardware shop. The sign was at the end of the street.

He stopped at the door.

Through the glass panel, Ray was sitting at his desk. The metal box was open in front of him. Ray held a piece of paper over a small, bright flame. A lit match. His fingers were steady.

He was about to destroy it.

Luca pushed the door hard and went in.

The smell of the match reached him first. Burnt. Sharp. The match was out now. Ray looked up. His face did not change.

"Leave," Ray said. His voice was flat.

"Agnes Ward told me everything," Luca said.

Ray put the document face-down on the desk. His hand stayed on top of it. He was a careful man. He waited.

"You have nothing," Ray said.

"I have the tape," Luca said.

"A tape proves nothing."

"Then why did you take the box from my flat?" Luca said. "Admit it. You took it."

Ray said nothing. He looked at the window. He looked at the floor. He did not look at Luca.

Luca set the video camera on the desk. He kept his hand on it.

"This tape is evidence," he said. "Evidence that someone hid the box. I can go to the police in ten minutes. Or you can give me the box right now."

Ray stared at the camera. He wanted to destroy the document. But the tape was in Luca's hand. He wanted to stop Luca. But he had nothing.

The consequences were close. He knew it.

Ray picked up the document. He placed it back in the metal box. He closed the lid. Then he pushed the box slowly across the desk.

He did not say the words. But his hands admitted everything.

Then Ray reached into his desk drawer. He took out a small piece of paper and set it next to the box.

"I always knew where she was," he said. His voice was quiet and flat. "The granddaughter. She lives in Fenwick. That is her address."

He paused. "I kept it for fifty years. I watched. I wanted to be ready."

"Give it to her," Ray said. "Then it is finished."

Luca picked up the paper, the metal box, and the camera. He walked out.

---

Fenwick was a small town with one long road and grey stone walls. The bus dropped Luca near the post office. He walked for five minutes and found the house — a low building with a brown door and a small garden.

He knocked.

A woman answered. She was older than Luca but not old. She had short grey hair and dark eyes. She looked at the metal box in his hands.

"My name is Luca Marr," he said. "This box belonged to your family. Someone kept it from them for a long time."

He held out the box.

She took it. She opened it slowly. She read the signed document. She held it in both hands and said nothing.

"You can return it to wherever you want," Luca said. "It is yours now."

She looked at him. "Thank you," she said. Her voice was very quiet.

Luca nodded. Then he left.

---

He walked back down the road toward the bus stop. The morning was still cold but the sky was bright. The box was back with the Cole family now. The evidence was in the right hands.

The consequences were waiting for Ray Brennan and Agnes Ward.

Luca walked on. For the first time since he found the camera in that dusty back room, he did not need to know anything more. The answer was in her hands.
