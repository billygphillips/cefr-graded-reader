The morning was grey and cold. Kai stood on the pavement outside Mrs. Webb's house. Her front yard was full of old tools and broken things. A rusted bucket sat near the gate. He could see a crowbar leaning against the fence.

He walked up to the gate. Mrs. Webb was already outside. She had a wide, flat face and small eyes that missed nothing.

"Morning," Kai said. "Can I borrow your crowbar? I need it for a job."

She looked at him. Then she looked at the crowbar.

"What kind of cleaning job needs a crowbar?" she said.

"There are some old boards in the back room," Kai said. "I need to move them."

She did not speak for a moment. Then she walked to the fence and picked up the crowbar. It was old and rusty, orange-brown along its length. She held it out to him.

"That old house brings trouble," she said. "It always has."

Kai took the crowbar. He did not ask what she meant. He said thank you and walked away.

The front door of the Old House opened quietly. Kai stepped inside and stopped. He listened. The hallway was silent. He walked straight to the back room.

The room was cold and still. Thin grey light came through the window. Kai knelt at the cracked floorboard in the corner and slid the rusty crowbar under its edge.

He pushed down hard and tried to force the board up. It did not move. He moved the crowbar a little and tried again. He pushed harder. His arms shook with the effort.

Then a loud creak came from directly above him.

Kai stopped. He sat very still. He looked at the doorway.

The house was quiet.

He waited. The creak did not come again. He told himself that old houses made sounds. But his hands moved faster now. He kept his eyes on the doorway.

He slid the crowbar further under the board and forced it down again. The nails groaned. He forced again. One nail lifted. Then another. The board came up with a sharp crack.

He set it aside and reached into the dark gap below. His hand touched cold metal.

He pulled out the box. It was heavy and covered in dust. It looked exactly like the box on the tape. He stood up. He tucked it under his arm and walked out of the back room. He did not stop. He did not look around.

Outside, Kai walked down the road until the Old House was behind him. Then he stopped.

He held the box in both hands and opened the lid.

Inside: a small key on a short metal chain. And an old photograph — two adults and a young child standing in front of the Old House. The sun was bright in their faces. They were all squinting.

Kai studied their faces. He did not know them.

He turned the lid over and looked at the inside surface. Letters were scratched into the metal, cut deep with something sharp.

H. O. L. T.

He read them twice. That was his employer's name.

The road was empty and quiet. Kai stood there. He looked at the photograph again — the two adults, the young child. Then he looked at the small key on its chain. He held it between his fingers and did not move.
