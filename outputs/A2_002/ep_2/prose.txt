Kai looked at the staircase for a long moment. The top was dark. He could not see anything up there. He thought about the drag mark on the floor behind him. He thought about the metal box.

He turned away from the stairs. The hallway was quiet. His footsteps were soft in the dust as he walked back to the back room.

He set his bag on the floor and took out the old camera. He turned on the small screen and pressed play.

The tape moved slowly. He watched the family again. The man. The woman. The child. They were all afraid. He could see fear in the way they moved — fast and low, close to the floor.

He pressed a button to slow the tape down. He watched the man kneel. The man knelt in the corner of the room, near the window. And there, just below the man's hands, was a thin crack running across one of the boards.

Kai stopped the tape.

He looked at the real floor. He walked to the corner nearest the window and crouched down. The wood was dry and cold under his fingers. He ran them along the boards, one by one. Then he found it. A thin crack, just like the one on the tape. It ran across the full width of the board.

This was the right one. He was certain.

He stood up and reached for his screwdriver. Then he stopped.

Near the cupboard, in the dust, he saw a footprint. It was wide and flat. He looked down at his own boot and pressed it into the dust beside the other print. His print was narrower. The footprint near the cupboard was not his.

Kai stood still. He listened. The house was silent. Someone had been in this room after his last visit.

He picked up his phone and called Mr. Holt.

The phone rang twice. Then Holt answered.

"Is everything all right?" Holt said.

"Yes," Kai said. "I just have a question. Does anyone else have a key to this house?"

There was a short pause. "No," Holt said. "Only you. Why?"

"I noticed some marks in the dust. In the back room."

"It's an old house," Holt said. "There's dust everywhere. Finish the job."

"But the marks look—"

"Kai." Holt's voice was flat. "Finish the job."

The call ended.

Kai put his phone in his pocket. He looked at the footprint again. Then he crouched down beside the cracked board.

He put the tip of the screwdriver into the edge of the board and pushed hard. The board did not move. He tried again, pushing down with both hands. Nothing. The board was tight. It would not lift.

He needed a crowbar. A crowbar could get under the edge and force the board up. But he did not have one. He had not brought one.

Kai sat back on his heels and looked at the crack in the board. The box was down there. He was sure of it. But he could not reach it today.

He put the screwdriver back in his bag. He stood up. He looked once more at the wide, flat footprint near the cupboard door. Whoever made it had stood in almost the same spot where Kai was standing now. They had looked at the same floor.

Kai picked up his bag and left the house.
