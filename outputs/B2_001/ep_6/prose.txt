They walked quickly. Mrs. Holt kept pace without complaint, her coat buttoned to the collar. Jamie had the camera in his bag and one woman's memory of a night fifty years ago. He needed to secure both before Voss had time to act.

The duty officer at Garfield Road listened without interrupting. Mrs. Holt gave her account clearly: the Warner family leaving with bags in the early hours, the fire an hour later, Robert Voss's car at the kerb — a name she confirmed she had known and feared. Jamie showed the camera footage. On the small screen, Voss walked straight to the far corner of the room and went directly to the floorboard location.

"It confirms she had prior knowledge of the layout," Jamie said.

The officer set the camera down. "The footage helps. But the documents in that locked drawer — I can't simply retrieve them from a private office without proper grounds. A case this old, with the primary suspect deceased, will take time to assess."

Jamie had expected something like this. Then Mrs. Holt spoke again.

"There is something I haven't told anyone," she said. She placed her hands flat on the table. "The morning after the fire, Robert Voss came to my door. He said: 'What you think you saw last night, Mrs. Holt — you didn't see it. And it would be a great shame if your family had the same trouble as the Warners.'"

The room was quiet.

"Those are his exact words?" the officer asked.

"I have been hearing them for fifty years," she said.

He wrote them down. "Threatening a witness isn't bound by the age of the original crime," he said. "There are consequences to words like those — and they connect directly to the evidence you've described." He stood. "I think we should go and see Miss Voss."

Jamie felt something loosen in his chest. Not quite relief yet, but close.

Voss was still at her desk. She looked up when they came in, and her face went still — not the way it usually did when she was choosing her words, but differently. She looked at the officer, not at Jamie or Mrs. Holt.

"These are old property records," she said. "I found them during a routine inspection. I had intended to hand them in."

The officer said nothing. Mrs. Holt spoke quietly.

"Your father stood on my doorstep," she said. "He said: 'What you think you saw last night, Mrs. Holt — you didn't see it. And it would be a great shame if your family had the same trouble as the Warners.'"

Voss did not speak.

The officer asked her to cooperate by making the drawer available. He said her cooperation would be noted. Voss reached into her jacket pocket and took out the small key. She had run through the consequences and made her calculation. She set the key on the desk, unlocked the drawer, and placed the note, the photographs, and the opened metal box in front of him, one by one, without a word.

The officer retrieved each item and bagged them, securing them as evidence. He told Voss that the inquiry would be in contact and she should remain available. Voss sat with her hands in her lap and said nothing.

Outside, the air had cooled. Jamie stood on the pavement and let the quiet settle.

He had found the camera in the cupboard, wrapped in cloth and tucked behind a rag — the kind of place a clearance worker would reach into first. The box had been under the floorboard in the room below. Not hidden together. Separate. Deliberate.

The camera exposed nothing on its own. But it pointed. Whoever watched it would see exactly which corner of the room mattered, which floorboard to lift. Under that floorboard, the proof.

Frank Warner had built a trail designed to expose the truth, even if he was never able to speak for himself. He had placed the camera to be found first, so that whoever found it would know exactly where to look next. Both items had been waiting in the dark for fifty years.

Mrs. Holt stood beside him, and the tension had left her face. He thought he understood something of what that kind of relief felt like, after fifty years of silence.

The inquiry was open. The truth was on the record. But somewhere, the Warner family had carried on without knowing that anyone had finally found what Frank left behind — or that, after all this time, someone had been listening.
