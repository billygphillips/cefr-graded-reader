Jamie knocked.

He heard slow footsteps, and then the door opened a few inches. Mrs. Holt looked at him through the gap, her expression the same as before: closed, cautious.

"I told you—" she began, and started to push the door shut.

"Voss has taken everything." He kept his voice steady. "The note, the photographs, the box. She locked them in her desk this morning. This camera is all I have left."

He held up the old camera so she could see the scratched casing. Mrs. Holt looked at it without speaking. Something shifted in her face — not pity, but the recognition of a specific kind of trouble.

She stepped back and let him in.

The front room was small and neat, with two armchairs and a gas fire that wasn't lit. Jamie sat where she indicated and placed the camera on the low table between them. He told her, as briefly as he could, what had happened at Voss Estate Agency: that Voss had admitted her father had planned the arson deliberately, that she had taken all the evidence, and warned him to say nothing.

"She admitted it?" Mrs. Holt said.

"She said Robert Voss was her father. Dead for twenty years. She seemed to think that made it finished."

Mrs. Holt went still. When she finally spoke, her voice was low and careful, as if she had been waiting a long time to say the words.

"I was watching the street that night," she said. "February. I couldn't sleep. I saw the Warners come out of the house in the dark — the whole family. Bags, one each. They didn't run, but they didn't look back either. They were fleeing, but quietly. It was deliberate. They knew exactly where they were going."

Jamie said nothing.

"About an hour after they left, the fire started. Front room. It went up fast." She paused, her hands flat on her knees. "There was a car parked at the kerb with its lights off. I knew that car. It belonged to Robert Voss — he'd been around Crane Street before, and everyone on the street knew him by sight."

She had been a witness to all of it — the family fleeing, the fire, the car — and she had kept it to herself for fifty years.

"The next morning he came to my door," she said. "He was polite about it. Said it would be better for everyone if I hadn't seen anything. I had a child of my own. I was frightened." She looked down at her hands. "I've been protecting myself ever since. I told myself I was protecting my family, but really it was myself."

"You're the only witness who was there," Jamie said. "If you gave a statement to the police, the camera footage would support it. It shows the box was hidden deliberately — that there was something to hide."

Before she could answer, there was a knock at the front door.

Neither of them spoke. Mrs. Holt went to answer it, and Voss stepped inside without waiting. She was dressed as she had been that morning — neat jacket, composed expression. Her voice was steady.

"I was just passing," she said to Mrs. Holt, as if this were a social call. Then she glanced at Jamie, and at the camera on the table. "Old footage of a man hiding a box doesn't prove anything about a crime committed fifty years ago. I'd think carefully before you take this anywhere."

She turned back to Mrs. Holt. "I don't want any difficulty for you. Voss Estate Agency has always tried to be straightforward with its tenants."

She didn't mention the rental agreement directly. She didn't need to.

Mrs. Holt looked at her for a long moment. Then she said, quietly and clearly: "I'll be giving a statement to the police about what I saw that night on Crane Street. I should have done it years ago."

Voss said nothing more. She looked at them both once, then walked out. The door clicked shut behind her.

Jamie moved to the window and watched her car pull away from the kerb and turn the corner at the end of the street. He knew exactly where she was going — back to her office and the locked drawer, with the note, the photographs, and the box all waiting inside it. She had the physical evidence. And she had a head start.

He turned to Mrs. Holt. "We need to go now," he said.
