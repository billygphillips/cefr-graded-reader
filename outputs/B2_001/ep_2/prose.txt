Jamie looked away from Mrs. Holt's window and made a decision before he had fully thought it through. He picked up the camera, walked out of the front door, crossed the narrow strip of path between the two houses, and knocked.

Slow footsteps. The door opened a crack. Mrs. Holt looked at him without speaking — a sharp-eyed woman with grey hair, her hand resting on the door frame.

"I'm clearing the house next door," Jamie said. "I found something."

She began to close the door. Jamie hesitated for half a second, then held the camera up and turned the small screen towards her. He pressed play.

Her hand stopped on the door frame. She leaned forward and watched — the front room of the empty house, a figure crouching by the bay window, pressing something beneath the floorboard. Then she straightened.

"Where did you find that?" she said.

"Hidden in a cupboard." Jamie kept his voice level. "Do you recognise him?"

She looked at the screen a moment longer, as though making sure. "That's Frank Warner."

Jamie waited. He was not sure he could convince her to say more if she chose to stop, so he said nothing and let the silence do the work.

"The Warners lived in that property for about six years," she said at last. "Frank, his wife, and their daughter — a young girl, maybe five or six." She paused. "Then one morning in 1974 they were gone. Overnight. No removal van, no goodbye. The whole family just — vanished."

"Did anyone ask questions?"

"Of course. But no one gave us answers." Her voice was flat, as though she had long since stopped expecting them. "The property sat empty after that. Years and years."

Jamie asked if she had any idea why the Warners had vanished, but Mrs. Holt shook her head once — firmly. He recognised the look that followed: not confusion, but a decision. She was done.

"If you find anything else," she said, "I'd keep it to yourself."

She closed the door.

Back in the front room, Jamie crouched by the bay window and pressed his fingers along the third floorboard's edge until he felt it give. He worked it up slowly. Beneath it, wrapped in a piece of old grey cloth, was a small metal box. He lifted it out — heavier than he had expected. The lock on the front was rusted solid, the kind that had not moved in years. He turned the box over, searching for any other clue — a name, a date, a mark — but the surface was plain and dark.

He hesitated a moment, the box resting in both hands. Then he placed it carefully at the bottom of his bag and folded the cloth around it so the lock would not catch on anything. He pressed the floorboard back down, smoothing along each edge until the gap was not obvious.

The room looked unchanged. Anyone coming in would find no clue that anything had been disturbed.

The knock at the front door came about twenty minutes later. Margaret Voss stood on the step, coat buttoned, a folder tucked under one arm.

"Just passing," she said. "Thought I'd check on progress."

"Going well," Jamie said.

She stepped inside without being invited. She looked around the front room — the bare walls, the stripped shelves. "Good. The new owners want the property cleared quickly." A small pause. "Nothing out of the ordinary, I hope? These old houses — people leave all sorts behind."

"Nothing unusual," Jamie said. He kept his voice even enough to convince her. Or to convince himself — he wasn't entirely sure which.

"Mm." Voss smiled and walked a little further into the room, the way someone does when they want to appear casual. Her eyes moved over the floorboards near the bay window. They found the exact spot and stayed there — one beat too long.

Then she looked up and smiled again.

Jamie's bag sat on the floor behind him, the metal box inside it, the rusted lock hidden under a layer of old cloth. He smiled back and said nothing. But he could not tell whether she already knew the box was gone.
