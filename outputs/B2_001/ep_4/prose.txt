Jamie sat for another minute on the bare floor, the bag beside him. Outside, a car passed slowly along Crane Street. The room felt smaller than it had an hour ago.

He couldn't stay here. Voss would come back, or she would send someone, and waiting while she decided her next move seemed worse than any other choice he could make. He wanted to confront her with the note while it was still in his hands — before she could take it and deny it had ever existed. It wasn't a plan. It wasn't even close to a plan. But he zipped the bag shut, stood up, and walked out of the house.

Voss Estate Agency was on the high street, eight minutes from Crane Street. He had noticed it on his first day — a narrow shopfront with a smart window display, the name printed in gold letters on the glass. He pushed the door open now and stepped inside. The office was small and tidy: two chairs for clients near the window, a filing cabinet against one wall, and at the back, a large desk with a lamp and an open laptop.

Margaret Voss was sitting behind the desk. She looked up when he came in. Her expression didn't change.

"Jamie," she said. "I wasn't expecting you."

He took the note and the three photographs from his bag and placed them on the desk in front of her. He had come here to confront her face to face, and he had expected her to deny everything — to say the note was meaningless, that she had never seen it, that he was making too much of old paper. She didn't. She looked at the photographs first, then at the note. Her hands stayed completely still.

"I went into the house before your first day," she said, in the same even tone she might use to describe a routine viewing. "I found the loose floorboard and searched beneath it. I ran out of time and left before I could take the box."

He stared at her. There it was.

"I want an explanation," he said. It was meant as a demand, but the pressure of the silence in the room had taken most of the force out of it.

She picked up the note and read it. She took her time. When she set it down, her face had not moved.

"R. Voss is my father," she said. "Robert Voss. He has been dead for twenty years." She paused. "I'm not going to deny that the note is real. But it doesn't tell the full story. You don't understand what you're involved in, and I won't allow his reputation — or mine — to be damaged by something taken out of context."

She opened her desk drawer and took out a white envelope. She slid it across the desk toward him. It was noticeably thick.

"The job is finished," she said. "Go home."

Jamie looked at the envelope. He hadn't expected the money to feel like pressure, but it did — sitting there between them, plain and businesslike, as if the conversation were already over. The truth was that coming to her office had been a bluff. He had arrived with the note in his hands and no idea what to do beyond putting it down in front of her, and she had shown him, calmly, exactly how little that was worth. She hadn't threatened him directly — no raised voice, no warnings he would later regret. Her controlled quiet was more threatening than anger would have been.

He demanded nothing more. He left the envelope where it was.

Voss didn't wait. She collected the note, the photographs, and the box from the desk, placed them in the drawer, and turned the small key in the lock. She dropped the key into her jacket pocket.

"Asking questions about 1974 won't help you," she said. "Or anyone else. I expect not to hear from you again."

Jamie picked up his bag and walked out.

On the pavement, the afternoon light was flat and ordinary. He stood still for a moment. The bag felt wrong — too light, almost empty. The camera was still inside, wrapped in its cloth. She had taken everything off the desk. But she had never asked about the camera. She didn't know it was there.

He turned back toward Crane Street. The bluff had cost him the note, the photographs, and the box. Her father's reputation was worth protecting, apparently. But she had said it herself: the note was not the full story.

When he reached Mrs. Holt's door, he stopped. He raised his hand to knock.
