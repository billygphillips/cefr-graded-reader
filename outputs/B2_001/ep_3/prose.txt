Voss brought her eyes back to Jamie's face. The smile was still there.

"I just wanted to check in," she said. "The house has been empty a long time. Sometimes people find things — odd little objects, that sort of thing." She paused. "Have you come across anything unusual?"

Jamie kept his face still. Behind him, the bag sat on the bare floor.

"Just old rubbish," he said. "A few bits of broken furniture. Nothing worth keeping."

Voss held the pause — one beat, two beats — before nodding. "Good. I'd like everything cleared by Friday." She moved toward the door. "And if you do find anything — anything at all — call me straight away. Don't wait."

Her voice was pleasant. The instruction wasn't.

As she stepped into the hallway, her eyes passed across the floor. The bag. She said nothing about it. Then the front door opened and closed, and Jamie stood listening to her footsteps on the path outside.

He didn't move until he heard her car start. He crossed to the window and watched it turn at the bottom of Crane Street and disappear.

Then he knelt beside the bag.

He unwrapped the box from the grey cloth and set it on the floorboard. The lid was sealed — rust had worked into the lock and packed the thin gap around the seam. He reached into the tool bag and found the flat-head screwdriver. He pressed its tip along the edge of the lid. The metal didn't move. He tried again with more force. The box shifted in his grip but held. He changed the angle, worked the screwdriver deeper, and pushed.

The lock gave with a dull crack.

He opened the lid. The contents surprised him. Inside, wrapped in a square of folded cloth, were three black-and-white photographs and a small envelope. He set the envelope aside and began examining the photographs one by one.

They all showed the front of a house — this house. But in the photographs, the windows were dark with smoke marks and the plaster around the frames was scorched. The damage was severe. In the third photograph, two figures stood near the front step, both with their faces turned away from the camera.

He set the photographs down and picked up the envelope. It had been sealed shut and was slightly swollen, the paper soft from years of damp. He opened it carefully along the top edge and unfolded the single sheet inside.

The handwriting was tight and slanted. The ink had faded to brown. At the top of the page, a date: 14 February 1974. Below it, a name: R. Voss.

He looked for a signature further down, but there wasn't one — just eight lines of close writing. He examined each line carefully. The contents of the note were plain enough. The writer described an arrangement between two parties to clear the Crane Street property. The method was fire — arson, planned deliberately while the Warners were still living on this street. The note said they would leave before it happened.

He read it again.

It was evidence of arson — real and readable, fifty years old. And the name written at the top of the page was Voss. The same name as the woman who had stood in this room twenty minutes ago and asked him, with her pleasant voice, whether he had found anything unusual.

He turned the sheet over. No address, no second signature. Nothing else.

Jamie set the note down beside the photographs and looked at them together. The scorched walls. The two figures with their backs to the camera. He was holding physical evidence of a plan to burn this house down, and the name on the note was Voss.

He sat still for a moment. Then he thought about what she had said as she walked out: *Call me straight away. Don't wait.*

She had looked at the bag before she left. He had told her there was nothing, and she had nodded and smiled and moved on. He wasn't sure she had believed him.

He stayed where he was, sitting on the bare floor with the note in his hand. He knew what she was looking for. He was beginning to think she already suspected he had it.
