The house on Crane Street smelled of old wood and damp. Jamie pulled on his work gloves and stepped through the front door, dragging a rubbish bag behind him. The hallway was narrow and dark, with wallpaper peeling away from the plaster in long strips. He had been told the house had stood empty for many years, and it felt like it.

He moved into the front room. A bay window faced the street, its glass filmed with dirt. The fireplace grate was rusted, and beside it an old wooden cupboard stood against the wall, its door hanging slightly open. Jamie started with the easier things — a broken chair, a stack of damp newspapers, a crate full of nothing worth keeping. He worked steadily, filling the bag without stopping.

When he opened the cupboard door fully, he found only bare shelves and a folded rag on the bottom. He was about to close it again when he noticed something tucked behind the rag — a small object wrapped tightly in a piece of dark cloth. He picked it up. It was heavier than it looked. He unwrapped it carefully and found a video camera, old but compact. One corner of the casing had a long scratch across the plastic, as if it had been dropped at some point.

The battery indicator was dead. He pulled the power bank from his bag and connected the camera to charge. While it charged, he sat on the floor with his back against the wall and looked at the ceiling.

Ten minutes later there was enough battery to press play.

The footage was grainy and the colour was washed out, but it was clear enough to follow. A man stood in what was obviously this same room — the bay window was visible in the background. He was carrying something rectangular, wrapped in a bag. He crouched near a floorboard at the far side of the room, close to the window, and worked carefully at its edge with a flat tool. He lifted the board, set it aside, placed the rectangular object into the gap below, and replaced the board, pressing it flat. Then he stood and turned, and for a moment he looked directly toward the camera. His face was visible — heavy jaw, dark eyes, middle-aged — but Jamie did not recognise him.

The footage ended there.

His phone buzzed. Margaret Voss.

"Jamie. How is the clearing going?"

"Fine," he said. "Making good progress."

"Good. I need everything out by the end of the week. All of it." Her tone was brisk, the kind that did not expect to be argued with. "There is a viewing scheduled for next Friday."

"Understood. I'll have it done."

"Call me if there are problems."

She ended the call. Jamie stood with the phone in his hand for a moment. He had said nothing about the camera. He hadn't made a decision about that — the word "fine" had simply come out before he had thought about it, and then the moment was gone.

He crossed the room toward the bay window. The floorboard from the footage — he was sure it was the right one, third from the wall, slightly wider than the others — was directly in front of him. He crouched down to investigate. The thick dust that covered every other board in the room had been wiped away from this one. Along its left edge, a line of fresh scratch marks ran from one end to the other, pale wood showing through clearly. He ran his finger along them. They were not old. The surrounding dust lay flat and undisturbed, the way dust looks when nothing has moved it in years — but this board had been disturbed recently. Weeks ago at most, not the decades the rest of the house suggested.

Someone had come here after the camera recorded the hiding. They had come to investigate, had found the right board — and then, for some reason, they had left without taking whatever was beneath it.

Jamie tucked the camera under his arm and straightened up. Through the bay window he could see the street and the house next door. A woman was standing at that window, absolutely still, watching him. She was older, with short grey hair and a sharp, steady expression. She did not wave. She did not look away. She simply stood there, watching, as if she had been at that window long before Jamie had ever arrived.

He looked down at the camera in his hand, then back up at her.

She still hadn't moved.
